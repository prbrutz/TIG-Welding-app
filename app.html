07 When should you pulse?

◆ Pulsing earns its keep

  • Thin sheet & burn-through risk. The cool background phase lets the puddle freeze slightly between peaks, so you pour less total heat into the part.
  • Controlling distortion & warp. Lower average heat means less expansion — a big help on stainless and thin aluminum.
  • Out-of-position work. Vertical and overhead beads stay put: the puddle solidifies on the background phase instead of sagging or dripping.
  • Steady cadence & "stacked-dime" beads. Low-frequency pulse gives you a metronome — dip the filler on each peak for an even, repeatable look.
  • Poor fit-up & gaps. The puddle stays stiffer, so it bridges gaps instead of falling through.
  • Heat-sensitive assemblies. Welding near bearings, electronics, thin-to-thick joints, or anything you don't want to cook.
  • Arc control (high frequency). Fast pulsing constricts and stiffens the arc for cleaner starts, a narrower deeper bead, and grain refinement.

◇ Skip it / no real gain

  • Thick plate needing max heat. When you want sustained penetration and a hot, fluid puddle, the background phase just slows you down — constant current is simpler.
  • High deposition / fast fill. The cooling cycle lowers deposition rate, so big fill passes go faster on steady current.
  • Flat-position welds you already control. If your puddle and travel are dialed in, pulse adds knobs without adding much benefit.
  • While learning the fundamentals. Torch angle, arc length and filler timing come first — extra pulse parameters can distract from the basics.
  • Simple, forgiving joints. Heavy lap or fillet welds on mild steel rarely need the extra control.
08 Low vs high frequency — two different jobs
0.5 – 10 PPS
Low frequency · thermal control

Slow enough that your eye and hand follow each pulse. This is the thermal tool: freeze the puddle between peaks for thin metal, distortion control, out-of-position beads, gap bridging, and a visible cadence for timing filler dips. Most hand TIG lives around 0.5–3 PPS.

100 – 500+ PPS
High frequency · arc control

Far too fast to track by eye. This is the arc tool: rapid switching constricts and stiffens the arc cone, giving better arc starts, a narrower and deeper weld, plus puddle agitation that refines grain and helps degas aluminum. Common in automated, orbital and precision work.

◆ Amperage or voltage — which do you actually pulse?

In TIG you pulse the amperage (current). TIG is a constant-current process: you set the current, and arc voltage simply follows your arc length (hold a longer arc and voltage rises on its own). There's no voltage set-point to pulse, so machines pulse between a peak and a background current.

Pulsed voltage is a MIG/MAG (GMAW) concept — that process is constant-voltage and uses a pulsed-voltage waveform to control metal transfer. So if a TIG menu mentions pulsing, it means current, which is why the calculator above works entirely in amps.

Peak amps
The hot phase that melts and penetrates. Start near your normal constant-current amperage for the thickness.
Background amps
The cool phase that lets the puddle freeze. Typically 20–40% of peak — lower = stronger freeze effect.
Frequency (PPS)
Pulses per second (= Hz). Low for thermal/cadence control, high to stiffen the arc.
Peak time %
Fraction of each cycle spent at peak. Higher % = more heat & penetration; ~40–60% is a common start.
AC balance (EN %)
For aluminum/mag: more EN = penetration & speed; more EP = oxide cleaning (needs a balled tip).
AC frequency (Hz)
Polarity-switch rate. Higher = a tighter, focused arc; lower = a wider, softer puddle.
General starting-point reference — always verify against your WPS, machine chart and material certs.
